Abstract

Patriot Enterprises LLC, a mid-tier federal GovCon providing professional services, IT support, and management consulting, produces proposals manually at approximately 8 per month with a 20% win rate. This project designs and prototypes an AI-assisted proposal generation system using a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture [2,3]. The system automates the most time-intensive early proposal stages — compliant outline construction, win theme development, and first-draft narrative — while preserving mandatory human decision authority at every step. Projected outcomes include a 30–40% reduction in proposal drafting time, capacity for additional bid volume without headcount expansion, and a projected win rate improvement to 25%+. The prototype is built on FAISS vector search, local embeddings via llama.cpp, and Claude hosted on Amazon Bedrock via Amazon GovCloud, routed through LiteLLM for compliance.

Introduction

The federal government obligates ~$744.7 billion annually in contracts governed by FAR and agency supplements including DFARS [1]. For GovCon firms, proposals are the sole mechanism for new revenue — yet the development process is expensive, regulated, and heavily dependent on skilled writer time.

Patriot faces a structural capacity constraint. With a 20% win rate and 8 proposals per month, every additional proposal requires either overtime from existing staff or a reduction in quality. Proposal writers spend the majority of their time on formatting, compliance matrices, and boilerplate narrative — tasks with low strategic value — leaving insufficient capacity for competitive differentiation and win theme development.

Research links proposal quality directly to award probability, and AI-assisted first-draft generation has been shown to reduce cognitive load and free writer attention for high-value activities [2,4]. This project applies RAG-based generation — the leading approach for knowledge-intensive document production [3] — to Patriot's specific institutional context.

Conclusions
  • Volume is the primary lever. More proposals at a constant win rate yields more wins mathematically. AI-assisted drafting removes the bottleneck without adding headcount.
  • Proprietary knowledge is the differentiator. Patriot's past performance, CO relationships, and competitive intelligence — indexed in the vector store — cannot be replicated by competitors adopting generic AI tools.
  • Human oversight is architecture, not policy. Review gates are hard-wired into the pipeline. No output advances without sign-off. This directly addresses leadership's concern about autonomous AI errors in a legally-exposed, FAR-regulated environment.
  • Build over buy. Custom RAG pipeline reaches working prototype in ~1 week. Enterprise platform integrations require 2–3 months and still lack Patriot-specific institutional context.
  • Future work: Production FAISS deployment, chunking strategy optimization, CO preference learning, evaluator feedback integration. Step 4 (mock SSB review) remains an open ethical question, intentionally excluded from current scope.

Methods & Materials

The system implements a three-stage RAG pipeline targeting the highest-labor phases of Patriot's proposal cycle. All AI outputs require human review and approval before advancing. Agentic autonomous flows are explicitly excluded at the architecture level — leadership identified unreviewed AI output as unacceptable risk in FAR-regulated procurement [5].

Discussion

The primary competitive advantage of this approach is Patriot's proprietary institutional knowledge. Generic AI tools — commercial platforms or off-the-shelf integrations — operate without access to Patriot's past performance, CO relationship data, or incumbent intelligence. The RAG architecture makes this knowledge retrievable at generation time, creating a capability that competitors cannot replicate simply by adopting the same tools [2,3].

The deliberate exclusion of autonomous flows is a strategic choice, not a technical limitation. Every human review gate is an architecture-level control, not a soft guideline. This design reflects a calibrated position: maximize AI leverage on low-risk, high-effort tasks while maintaining human accountability on all outputs that carry legal or competitive exposure.

The deskilling limitation — writers atrophying core skills through over-reliance on AI drafts — is the structurally weakest mitigation in the design. AI-off exercises lack enforcement mechanisms unlike all other pipeline controls. This gap is named explicitly rather than treated as a solved problem.

Known Limitations
  • Retrieval quality dependency — output quality degrades if the knowledge base is sparse or improperly chunked
  • Hallucination risk — LLMs produce plausible but false claims; human review is non-negotiable, not optional
  • Chunking sensitivity — segmentation strategy significantly affects retrieval relevance and section coherence
  • Deskilling (design gap) — AI-off exercises lack enforcement; this is structurally weaker than all other architecture-level controls and is named explicitly
  • Untested prototype — all outcome projections are estimates; pilot validation is required before production
